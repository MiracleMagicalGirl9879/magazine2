Teens, Screens, and Mental Health: A WHO Perspective

The World Health Organization’s recent findings on adolescent screen use highlight both the opportunities and risks of digital engagement. While social media and gaming can foster connection and entertainment, problematic use is increasingly linked to mental health challenges, sleep disruption, and academic difficulties. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for developing effective interventions that balance the benefits of technology with the need to protect adolescent well-being.

The WHO’s Health Behaviour in School-aged Children (HBSC) study revealed that problematic social media use among adolescents rose from 7% in 2018 to 11% in 2022. Girls reported higher rates than boys, with 13% experiencing addiction-like symptoms compared to 9% of boys. These symptoms include withdrawal when offline, neglect of daily responsibilities, and negative consequences in school or relationships. Such patterns are strongly associated with depression, anxiety, and reduced academic performance, underscoring the urgent need for digital literacy education and parental guidance.

Gaming is another area of concern. The study found that 34% of adolescents play daily, with 22% gaming for at least four hours on those days. While gaming can provide social connection and enjoyment, 12% of adolescents are at risk of problematic gaming, with boys disproportionately affected. Research links excessive gaming to lower life satisfaction, more frequent psychological complaints, and weaker peer support. These findings suggest that while gaming can be a positive outlet, it requires careful monitoring to prevent harmful consequences.

While social media and gaming can foster connection and enjoyment, their problematic use poses significant risks. The path forward lies in equipping young people with digital literacy skills, providing accessible mental health support, and fostering open dialogue between families, schools, and communities. By striking this balance, society can ensure that technology serves as a tool for empowerment rather than a source of harm.
