What I've learned and unlearned about self-worth on social media

Screens have become an inseparable part of modern family life, often replacing conversation and connection with digital absorption. While mobile devices offer convenience and entertainment, their overuse has raised concerns about sleep disruption, emotional well-being, and even “screen dependency disorder.” Governments are beginning to regulate platforms, but lasting solutions depend on families themselves modeling healthier habits.

It is now common to see both adults and children glued to their devices during meals, in traffic, or while waiting in public spaces. Smartphones and tablets have shifted from tools into companions and pacifiers, sometimes even substituting for parental attention. Studies show that teenagers spend an average of 7.5 hours per day on smartphones, while younger children log nearly five hours. Adults are not far behind, averaging more than six hours daily. This widespread dependence highlights how screens have become “family members” in their own right.

Excessive screen time has measurable effects on both mental and physical health. Blue light interferes with melatonin production, impairing sleep quality. Cognitive development in young children is at risk, with prolonged exposure linked to reduced focus, slower learning, and even altered brain structure. Behavioral problems such as aggression, poor emotional regulation, and social isolation are increasingly documented. Social media compounds these risks by creating addictive validation loops, where children derive self-worth from likes and followers.

Recognizing the scale of the problem, governments are beginning to act. In the United States, New York’s Attorney General has proposed regulations requiring platforms to verify user age, block algorithm-driven feeds for minors without parental consent, and restrict notifications between midnight and 6 a.m. under the SAFE for Kids Act. Australia is going further, banning children under 16 from opening social media accounts starting December 2025, with fines of up to AUD 50 million for violations. These measures aim to curb addictive behavior, though enforcement remains challenging given the power of tech companies and ingrained digital habits.

Ultimately, laws can only do so much. Families must take responsibility by modeling the behavior they want to see putting phones down during dinner, encouraging offline activities, and creating safe spaces for real-world connection. Screens and social media will remain part of modern life, but they must be managed, not worshipped. The challenge is to ensure that children grow up emotionally connected, physically active, and mentally balanced, rather than overstimulated and isolated by constant digital engagement.
